# Proposal Anatomy

The section-by-section shape of a proposal that wins with this reader. Used to spot missing pieces (a finding can point at a gap, not just a line). Order matters.

## 1. Header + meta
Vendor name, prepared-for, prepared-by, date, and an expiration date. The expiration date is not a pressure gimmick — it's what makes "later" feel like a decision instead of a default.

## 2. Where things stand (their problem)
2–4 sentences on the prospect's current situation, specific and checkable: what their site has and lacks, what their review profile says, what a real search for their service shows today. Ends with why the gap is winnable (competitor weakness), not with fear.

## 3. The plan, phased
Phase 1 = the one-time build (scoped, week-counted). Phase 2 = the recurring program (monthly, countable deliverables). Each bullet is something the owner could verify happened. Plain words; any trade term gets its payoff in the same sentence.

## 4. Investment
A small table: item, one-line description, price. One recommended structure; at most one alternative framing (e.g., no-upfront monthly on a longer term). Recurring items state the commitment period. The word is "investment" only if the next section earns it.

## 5. Why this pays for itself
The arithmetic: average job/policy/case value (sourced) × margin or commission × conservative volume, landing on "roughly N new customers per month covers the whole program." One paragraph. Sources linked inline.

## 6. Next steps
One action, one sentence, one time anchor: "Reply or call to confirm, and the new site is live within four weeks."

## Global properties

- Two pages of substance, maximum
- Zero paragraphs about the vendor beyond one identity line in the header
- Every number either sourced, or about the prospect and checkable
- No guarantee of any outcome a third party's algorithm controls
