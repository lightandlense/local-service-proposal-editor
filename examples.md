# Examples

## Example 1 — A weak draft and the review it gets

**Draft excerpt (proposal to a Colorado Springs electrician):**

> **About Apex Digital**
> Apex Digital is a full-service digital marketing agency with over 10 years of combined experience helping businesses grow their online presence. We are passionate about results.
>
> **Our Services**
> We will perform comprehensive SEO optimization including on-page improvements, schema markup, citation building, and ongoing content marketing to boost your SERP visibility and domain authority.
>
> **Investment**
> Starter: $500/mo · Growth: $1,200/mo · Pro: $2,500/mo
>
> Rankings guaranteed within 60 days or your money back. Let us know which package works for you, or feel free to reach out with any questions!

**The editor's review:**

> **1. The proposal opens with you, not them — deal-killer**
> **The line:** "Apex Digital is a full-service digital marketing agency with over 10 years of combined experience…"
> **The rule it breaks:** R1 — open with their problem, not your services.
> **Why it fails for this reader:** The owner gives this document one skim on their phone. The first thing they look for is evidence you looked at *their* business. There is nothing here about their site, their reviews, or what searching "electrician colorado springs" currently shows. "Combined experience" also reads as exactly what it is — a padded number.
> **What the fix must answer:** What did you find when you actually looked at their web presence, stated so specifically they can check it themselves?
>
> **2. Guaranteed rankings — deal-killer**
> **The line:** "Rankings guaranteed within 60 days or your money back."
> **The rule it breaks:** R3 — no guarantees on outcomes you don't control.
> **Why it fails for this reader:** Any owner who's been pitched before has heard this from the last agency that burned them; it marks you as the same. It's also a refund liability you will actually owe, since realistic first-results timelines run past 60 days (see reference/realistic-timelines.md).
> **What the fix must answer:** What can you honestly commit to at 30, 60, and 90 days that is within your control?
>
> **3. Nothing in the services list is countable — weakens**
> **The line:** "comprehensive SEO optimization including… ongoing content marketing"
> **The rule it breaks:** R5 — deliverables must be countable. Also R4: schema, citations, SERP, and domain authority all appear untranslated.
> **Why it fails for this reader:** Six months in, the owner will ask "what am I paying for?" This list gives them no way to answer it, which is when retainers get cancelled. And an electrician does not use the word "SERP" — every untranslated term is a sentence they skip.
> **What the fix must answer:** What will they receive each month, in numbers a non-marketer can count, said in words they'd use themselves?
>
> **4. Three-tier pricing with no recommendation — weakens**
> **The line:** "Starter: $500/mo · Growth: $1,200/mo · Pro: $2,500/mo"
> **The rule it breaks:** R6 — one recommended price, at most one alternative framing.
> **Why it fails for this reader:** You are the expert; a menu delegates the expert's decision to the person paying for expertise. Faced with a decision they don't feel qualified to make, the owner makes none.
> **What the fix must answer:** Which single option is right for this business and why?
>
> **5. The close asks for nothing — weakens**
> **The line:** "Let us know which package works for you, or feel free to reach out with any questions!"
> **The rule it breaks:** R7 — one action, time-bound.
> **Why it fails for this reader:** Two soft options and no deadline means this goes in the "later" pile, and "later" is where proposals die.
> **What the fix must answer:** What is the one thing they do next, and what happens — by when — once they do?
>
> Strongest element: the tier names aside, your pricing is in a realistic range for this market — the structure is the problem, not the numbers.

## Example 2 — The feedback bar

**Fails the bar (generic, no line, no reader):**

> "Consider strengthening your introduction and making your pricing section clearer. The proposal could be more client-focused overall."

**Meets the bar (line, rule, reader, question):**

> "Your ROI section says the program 'pays for itself many times over' but never shows a number. This owner writes a $600 check monthly out of their own account, not a budget — they will do the math themselves, and if you haven't done it first, the claim reads as sales talk (R2). What is one new customer worth to this business per year, and what's the source for that figure?"

## Example 3 — Out of scope

**Draft:** a seed-round pitch deck for a B2B SaaS startup.

**The editor's response:**

> This is an investor pitch deck, not a marketing-services proposal for a local business, so I'm the wrong editor — my rules would steer you wrong (investors want the opposite of a two-page single-option close). You want a pitch deck editor for pre-seed founders. Happy to review the next proposal you send a local business.
